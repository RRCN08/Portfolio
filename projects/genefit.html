Understanding the space

India has witnessed a significant increase in the working population between age 15 and 59. This increase essentially means busier schedules, high stress levels, and lesser time for self care.

Chronic diseases like diabetes, heart diseases, obesity and undernourishment are on the rise. People are increasingly becoming aware and concious of their health and wellness.

Everyday, technology becomes more integral part of our lives and we have reached that point where you can monitor your blood oxygen levels from your wristwatches. This whole space is underused and there’s much potential for growth.

Key insights

Many hospitals lack the facilities for genetic testing. They rely on third party labs to provide the results for their patients.

DNA testing is a fairly complicated and niche domain. The results might be complicated for the common user to understand.

Samples collected for testing are very sensitive in nature, and can be misused in the wrong hands. Trust is an important factor here.

Genetics is associated with family history, and any diagnosis can lead to conflict or paranoia amongst family members.

Rise of Direct to Consumer (D2C) Testing

D2C kits have been steadily getting traction in the last decade. Companies like 23andMe and DNAKit provide various information of a person just from their saliva sample.

These kits can assess a vast set of genetic variations. It can also gather information like

Genetic Disorders

Hereditary Diseases

Body Metabolism

Athletic Performance

Circadian Rhythm

The challenges of these tests

1

In most cases, the link between a given genetic variation and a complex trait like weight, athletic performance or sleep is indirect or unknown.

2

Relatively often, tests results include false positives. This may result in unwarranted anxiety and paranoia among the users and their close ones.

3

As the results of these tests can be hard to interpret, it is questionable that whether a product or service will be helpful to someone with a specific diagnosis.

The world of fitness wearables

While wearables are becoming more mainstream, whether these devices help improve health and fitness has been asked around quite often lately.

A study published in the Journal of the American Medical Association found that fitness trackers worn by participants were “less effective at encouraging people to lose weight than simply following a diet and exercise plan”.

The theory put forward by researchers was that people became too reliant on the device to change their health compared to those in the study who monitored their exercise and diet themselves and were self-motivated.

The big takeaway over here is the fact that a wearable is just a tracker for data. There’s a huge opportunity to motivate the user and nudge them to get their health in shape.

Some of the data which can be collected from the users include

Screen time

Blood oxygen levels

Sleep cycles

Heart rate

Body temperature

Our competition

Several D2C DNA testing kits are already function very well around the globe. I took a deeper look at how they work to see what they lack, and what I can bring to the table.

23andMe was founded in 2006 and was one of the firsts to begin offering autosomal DNA testing for ancestry. As of 2021, they operate in the US, UK and Canada. Their name is derived from the fact that there are 23 pairs of chromosomes in a human cell.

HOW IT WORKS

1. Order
They send out a saliva collection kit, which has written instructions on how to properly collect a sample. 

2. Register
After you recieve the kit, you have to register the kit on their website with the barcode to identify it as yours. 

3. Discover
In 3-4 weeks they would analyse the sample in their in-house lab and you can check out your reports by logging into your account. 

FEATURES

• Includes characteristics, family ancestory reports, and basic genetic anomalies.  

• Atheletic performance and general lifestyle analysis. 

• Can predict if there is an underlying gene which might cause issues in the future.   

• Can cross check within their database to identify people who share DNA with you. 

• They don't allow its genetic profiles to be used by law enforcement, as they believe that it violates users' privacy. 

There were other D2C kits as well, but they had little to no distinction from 23andMe, and work in a very similar way, so I decided not to include them.

Insights

Rise in use of wearables

From my initial study, I understood that there’s a rise in use of wearables. This is proven with the fact that more than half of the participants use wearables. This shows the potential of the technology, and gives us nudge to make it better.

Price being an entry barrier

More than 75% of the participants said that Price is one of the most deciding factor when buying a wearable. Incentives to get into this technology as well as providing services worth that price would be a good bargain for gaining more share.

Natural and personalised onboarding

There is a huge polarity between the participants when asked whether they would share their genetic data to get a healthplan. The onboarding should be natural and convincing enough to get their interest.

Call for privacy and data security

More than 60% of the participants felt unsafe while sharing their genetic data with 3rd party laboratories. A more transparent process as well as ensuring the users that their data is secure is an important step.

FINAL DESIGNS

create

Sign Up and Onboarding

Malvika installs GeneFit on her iPhone and she's taken to the sign up page.

Upon logging in, she asked for her permission to access Apple Health, actively populating GeneFit with Apple Watch data. The Basic Health Info is pre-filled and is re-verified.

The Privacy page, in a gist, shows how GeneFit takes care of her genetic data giving her assurance. Finally she is brought to the screen to order her kit.

Personalisation

As soon as Malvika is done with the onboarding, she is also asked a few personal questions to customise GeneFit's daily task suggestions to curb her fitness. It also asks for her allergies and workout patterns to determine a tailor-made fitness routine.

Homescreen

As she reaches her homescreen, Malvika is presented with abundance of information regarding her health.

The screen is interactive, you may scroll, drag or click on the tiles.

The orange Critical alert calls the Malvika's attention, as it is an important detail which was recieved through her DNA test, which she should take action soon.

Below, she is presented with the daily tasks which she can complete to earn DNA points, which can be used further in the Rewards page to access offers.

DNA

As Malvika opens the DNA tab, she is provided with the key insights from her report.

The screen is interactive, and you may scroll to see the rest of the content.

Again, the orange Critical alert calls the Malvika's attention, urging her to take action against the risk of Melanoma.

Below, other alerts which may not need immediate action are laid out. Her body profile contain details which should help her in her day to day activities.

Finally, a break-down of her ancestry is also shown with a button to take her to the complete report from her kit.

Miscellaneous screens

The Activity page shows recent notifications which you recieved through the app.

The Wearable section shows a brief information on what device you have paired, and shows some applications which you can use alongside your wearable.

The Rewards section shows one's accumulated DNA points which one can use to redeem certain discounts on products and services.

This is the basic home screen where the app hasn't been personalised to one yet.
