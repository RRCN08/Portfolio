These test kits can make it seem like the future is here, and healthcare is reshaped entirely. While we may get there sometime in the near future, in the present world, these tests have quite a few downsides.

In most cases, the link between a given genetic variation and a complex trait like weight, athletic performance or sleep is indirect or unknown.

Relatively often, tests results include false positives. This may result in unwarranted anxiety and paranoia among the users and their close ones.

The results of these tests can be challenging to interpret, and whether a recommended product or service will be helpful to someone with a specific diagnosis can be questionable.

The world of fitness wearables

In 2015, the first-gen Apple Watch took the world by a storm. It was one of the most feature packed and expensive wearable till date. It went on to generate $9 billion in sales and the figures only increased in the coming years.

While wearables are becoming more mainstream, the question of whether these devices actually help in improving health and fitness has been has been asked around quite often lately.

A study, published in the Journal of the American Medical Association, found that fitness trackers worn by participants were “less effective at encouraging people to lose weight than simply following a diet and exercise plan”.

The theory put forward by researchers was that people became too reliant on the device to change their health compared to those in the study who simply monitored their exercise and diet themselves and self-motivated.

The big takeaway over here is the fact that a wearable is just a tracker for data. There’s a huge opportunity to provide motivation to the user and nudge them to get their health in shape.
